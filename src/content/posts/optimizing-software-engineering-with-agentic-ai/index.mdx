For better or worse, we love giving things new names. One of the latest terms being thrown around is harness engineering. However, with the possible exception of engineers building agent platforms, harness engineering is a fairly redundant framing for most teams adopting software engineering agents.

That doesn't mean agents don't require configuration, integration, or tooling. They do because LLMs are trained on generic software engineering artefacts rather than your specific engineering environment. Once connected to an engineering environment, agents reflect the strengths and weaknesses of the software engineering artefacts they find there. They also reveal how your team builds software: the assumptions, shortcuts, inconsistencies, and missing context that usually stay implicit.

The distinction is where the engineering effort should accumulate: in the same documentation, work definitions, implementation patterns, tests, and tooling that support people and software delivery, rather than in a parallel process that only the agent can use. This is the gap we need to bridge: between generic AI agents and the contexts they're used in.

So, if harness engineering's misleading, how should we be approaching this problem?

I'd argue a better framing would be to reevaluate and optimize existing software engineering practices with assistance from agentic AI.

That means looking at the engineering environment as a connected system: how work is understood, performed, checked, and improved. The most valuable changes should follow evidence rather than a generic checklist of agent-specific practices.

Agents can participate in that improvement process. The friction they encounter during real work can reveal where the environment is unclear, incomplete, or challenging to use. Instead of losing that evidence when a task ends, we can use it to identify, recommend, and validate durable improvements.

In this series, I'll start by examining the agentic AI stack and the engineering environment it depends on. From there, I'll explore how agents can help identify opportunities to improve that environment through existing software engineering practices.
